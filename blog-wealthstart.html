What I Learned Building a Fintech App Solo with Claude Code — From the PM Seat

6 min read

When I moved to the US for my MBA, the American financial system felt like a foreign language. I'd heard "401k" and "HSA" and "ROTH IRA" plenty of times — but actually knowing what to do with them, while managing student loans as an immigrant? Genuinely overwhelming. I did what most people do: figured it out slowly and wished someone had handed me a simple guide.

That frustration became WealthStart.

→ Live site  ·  GitHub

The other push was honestly a little fear. AI was everywhere, moving fast, and I kept reading about it as a PM without understanding what it actually felt like from the inside. I'm not a developer — the terminal screen has always been someone else's territory. But I was curious enough to try. So I did.

Here's what I actually learned.

1. The PRD is your prompt

When I started with a vague idea ("build a tax planning tool"), the output was vague. When I wrote a real PRD — target user, user journey, module specs, edge cases — everything clicked. But what surprised me was what happened next: Claude started acting less like a tool and more like a thought partner. It understood what I was trying to build well enough to push back sometimes — asking me to clarify who the user was, or flagging when a flow might confuse someone. There were moments where a question it asked made me rethink a decision I'd already made.

I didn't expect that. I expected a fast executor. I got something closer to a junior collaborator who'd actually read the brief.

2. AI handles execution. You still own the judgment.

Every meaningful product decision — what to build, what to cut, what order things happen in — was still mine. AI doesn't push back on bad ideas. It doesn't tell you your user flow is confusing or your copy is too jargon-y. It ships what you ask for, enthusiastically, at speed. The moment I treated it like a product manager rather than an executor, the output got generic fast.

The quality ceiling is set entirely by your ability to evaluate what it produces. That's a product skill, not a coding skill.

3. Speed changes what's hard, not what's important

The app took days instead of months. That part lived up to the hype. But the friction shifted — a lot of it came from tools and concepts I'd heard about at work for years but never had to deal with hands-on, because my engineers always handled them. Deployment pipelines. Dependency management. Authentication configuration.

What saved me was being able to ask Claude to explain them — not just fix them, but help me understand what was actually happening and why. Less "AI does the work" and more "AI helps me learn fast enough to keep moving."

What I'd do differently next time

There's an irony to building with AI: the speed is the feature, and the speed is the trap. Things come together so fast that you start shipping on instinct — skipping steps you'd never skip at work, convincing yourself it's fine because the thing works. It's not laziness. It's excitement. But the gaps it leaves are real.

  • I skipped testing entirely. Just manually clicking through flows and hoping for the best. In the excitement of getting something live, I convinced myself it was fine. It probably mostly is. "Probably mostly" isn't a real answer though.
  • Mobile was an afterthought I never came back to. It was on my PRD from day one. It's still a to-do. Classic case of shipping the thing you can see.
  • I went straight to deployment without a pre-launch checklist. What followed was a two-hour debugging session that a 15-minute audit would have caught. Lesson learned the slightly painful way.
