How I Delivered a 2-Month Project in 9 Days Using the BMAD Framework

5 min read

I'll be honest — for the first few months of using AI at work, I was basically winging it. Open a chat, type something in, hope for the best. It worked well enough that I kept doing it, but the results were inconsistent. Some days genuinely useful, other days I'd spend more time fixing the output than if I'd just done the thing myself.

Then I found BMAD, and something clicked.

What is BMAD?

BMAD (Build More Architect Dreams) is an open-source framework that brings structure to AI-assisted product delivery. It's a step-by-step workflow that covers the entire lifecycle — PRD, technical design, JIRA epics and stories, development, testing, deployment. The whole thing.

The idea is straightforward: vague input gives vague output. BMAD forces you to be specific at every step, which makes the AI dramatically more useful at every step.

9 days instead of 2 months

The proof for me was a product I delivered in a single sprint — 9 days — that had been scoped as a 2-month effort.

That's not because AI magically wrote everything. It's because BMAD front-loads the hard thinking. By the time I'd worked through the PRD and technical design steps, the requirements were tight enough that development moved fast with very little back-and-forth. Edge cases were already handled. Ambiguities were already resolved. The rework that usually eats weeks just... didn't happen.

For a PM working in a large org where re-iteration is expensive, that's the real win.

The Obsidian vault trick

One thing I've layered on top of BMAD: I keep my product's architecture in an Obsidian vault and give it to Claude as context at the start of every session.

Obsidian is a note-taking tool I use to document system architecture, data flows, and design decisions — the institutional knowledge that usually lives in someone's head. When Claude has that upfront, the output is noticeably sharper. It's working within the actual system, not guessing about it.

It's a small habit that made a noticeable difference.

What I took away

The biggest shift wasn't speed — it was quality. Tighter PRDs, fewer surprises in development, less time spent re-explaining context. BMAD didn't just make me faster. It made the whole delivery process less chaotic.

I'm still learning how to get the most out of it. But going back to winging it isn't something I'm considering.
